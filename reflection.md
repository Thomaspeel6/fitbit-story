# Why I made this

I keep thinking about the 2015 Connecticut case. A guy told police an intruder had killed his wife and tied him up. Her Fitbit said she was still walking around the house long after he said she was dead, and that's what got him charged. The watch she had bought for herself, for whatever personal reasons, ended up being a key witness in court. Nobody buys a fitness tracker planning for that.

That case is kind of the origin story for this project. I don't really worry about Fitbit as a thing that tracks steps. I worry about it the second the data stops being yours. Because once it leaves your body it isn't really health data anymore. It's evidence in a case, or a signal to an underwriter, or a row in somebody's quarterly report. The question I actually care about isn't whether wearables are good or bad. It's what happens when you aren't the only one looking at the numbers they produce.

# Why I made it a game

I picked an interactive story on purpose. When people read an essay about employer surveillance they nod and scroll. When they click "sign up, $50 is $50" in a browser and end up two clicks later looking at an HR email about their sleep data, something actually lands. I wanted the player to feel the thing, not just read about it.

The format is basically stolen from *Welcome to Vanguard Estates*. What that story does really well is make every choice feel reasonable when you make it and terrible by the end. I tried to do the same. None of the seven endings here happen because the player is reckless or greedy. They happen because the player is normal. They take the discount. They don't want to make a scene with their manager. They trust HR. They skip the 47-page PDF. Honestly, that's most of us.

# Authority

Authority was the 8KQ I leaned on hardest in my Week 13 discussion post and Lab #4, and it's the framework that runs through this story too. Linda has authority over the player, but it's just role-based. She didn't build the wellness program, she just enforces it. HR's only real expertise is that they're the people who actually read the terms of service, which is kind of a weird claim to authority when you say it out loud. Peel as a company has contractual authority, buried in a sentence about "de-identified aggregated biometric data." None of those feel clearly illegitimate on their own. That's kind of the problem. It's when you stack them up that it starts to look like a lot of the player's life is being run by people whose right to run it was never really earned.

The Organize ending is the only one where the player actually builds legitimate authority back, through coworkers and a letter. That wasn't an accident. We kept hitting this idea in class that position-based authority and expertise-based authority aren't the same thing, and that the real question is whether the authority was earned. Most of my endings let position-based authority win by default. The player has to actually do the work to get the other kind.

# What the branches are doing

There's a few ideas sitting underneath the branches that the story doesn't spell out. The Game the Data path is about what happens when a metric becomes a target. It stops being a useful metric, and people start gaming it. The brutal part of the Fitbit version is the company has already thought about gaming and written it into the TOS as fraud. A school juking its test scores at least doesn't get anyone charged with a crime.

The Steady Decline ending is about how fast surveillance stops feeling like surveillance. It's not the first time somebody notices the watch on your wrist during a performance review that bothers you, it's the fiftieth, when nobody notices anymore. Two years later it's just what onboarding looks like.

The Case ending is about the gap wearables sit in. They collect data that looks medical but isn't treated as medical legally, so if something goes wrong the options are slow, uneven, and usually end in an NDA.

# The one ending that's actually honest

The Quiet Cost is the ending most people, asked in the abstract what they'd do, would say is the right answer. You decline the program. You don't hand over your data. You keep your privacy. On paper that's the Liberty answer in the 8KQ framework, the player held onto their autonomy.

But in the story, declining isn't free. The player who picks that path pays full premiums every month, gets passed over for a promotion, and quietly absorbs the cost of staying out. I didn't want privacy to feel like a free choice, because in real life it isn't. In 2026, keeping your data to yourself costs you money, social capital, and sometimes a job. Privacy isn't the default anymore. It's a thing you pay for.

That's the part I wanted to be honest about. Every good ending in this story has a price. Clean Break costs you your job. Organize costs you time and political risk. Even The Quiet Cost, the seemingly easy opt-out, charges you every month. There's no path through the story where you walk away with no compromise, and that's on purpose.

If the game works the way I want, the player finishes one path and clicks back to try another. The first click is just a choice. The second one is where it gets uncomfortable, because now they've seen the cost of the path they didn't take and the cost of the one they did. There's nowhere clean to land. That's kind of the whole point.
