# Why I made this

I keep thinking about the 2015 Connecticut case. A guy told police an intruder had killed his wife and tied him up. Her Fitbit said she was still walking around the house long after he said she was dead, and that's what got him charged. The watch she had bought for herself, for personal reasons, ended up being a key witness in court. Nobody buys a fitness tracker planning for that.

That case is kind of the origin story for this project. I don't really worry about Fitbit as a thing that tracks steps. I worry about what happens the second the data stops being yours. Because once it leaves your body it isn't really health data anymore. It's evidence in a case, or a signal to an underwriter, or a row in somebody's quarterly report. The question I actually care about isn't whether wearables are good or bad. It's what happens when you aren't the only one looking at the numbers they produce.

# Why I made it a game

I picked an interactive story on purpose. When reading an essay about employer surveillance they may nod and scroll. When they click "sign up, $50 is $50" in a browser and end up two clicks later looking at an HR email about their sleep data, the player somewhat feels the impact.

The format is basically "stolen" from *Welcome to Vanguard Estates*. What that story does really well is make every choice feel reasonable when you make it and terrible by the end. I tried to do the same. None of the seven endings here happen because the player is reckless. They happen because the player acts as a rational human. They take the discount. They don't want to make a scene with their manager. They trust HR. They skip the 47-page PDF. Which is how most people operate.

# Authority

Authority was the 8KQ I leaned on hardest in my recent discussion posts and labs and it's the framework that runs through this story too. Linda has authority over the player, but it's just role-based. She didn't build the wellness program, she just enforces it. HR's only real expertise is that they're the people who actually read the terms of service, which is kind of a weird claim to authority, but is still authority. Peel as a company has contractual authority, buried in a sentence about "de-identified aggregated biometric data." None of those feel clearly illegitimate on their own. That's the problem. It's when you stack them up that it starts to look like a lot of the player's life is being run by people whose right to run it was never really earned.

The Organise ending is the only one where the player actually builds legitimate authority back, through coworkers and a letter. That wasn't an accident. We kept hitting this idea in class that position-based authority and expertise-based authority aren't the same thing, and that the real question is whether the authority was earned. Most of my endings let position-based authority win by default. The player has to actually do the work to get expertise-based.

# What the branches are doing

There's a few ideas sitting underneath the branches that the story doesn't spell out. The Game the Data path is about what happens when a metric becomes a target. It stops being a useful metric, and people start gaming it. The brutal part of the Fitbit version is the company has already thought about gaming and written it into the TOS as fraud. A student cheating in an exam at least doesn't get anyone charged with a crime.

The Steady Decline ending is about how fast surveillance stops feeling like surveillance. It's not the first time somebody notices the watch on your wrist during a performance review that bothers you, it's the fiftieth, when nobody notices anymore. Two years later it's just what onboarding looks like.

The Case ending is about the gap wearables sit in. They collect data that looks medical but isn't treated as medical legally, so if something goes wrong the options are slow, uneven, and usually end in an NDA.

# The one ending that's actually honest

The Quiet Cost is the ending most people would tell you is the right one. You opt out, you keep your data, you walk away. On paper that's the clean answer, the Liberty answer, the player keeps their autonomy.

But the story doesn't let it off the hook. The player who picks that path pays full premiums every month and gets passed over for a promotion they probably should've gotten. They didn't do anything wrong. They just kept their data to themselves, and that turns out to cost real money. In 2026, privacy isn't the default anymore, it's basically a paid tier.

That's the part I wanted to be honest about. Every ending in the story has a price tag. Clean Break costs you the job. Organise costs you time, and the political risk of being the person who started something. Even The Quiet Cost, which looks like the easy out, is quietly charging you every month forever. There's nowhere in this story you can land for free, and that's on purpose. If the game does its job, the player finishes one path, clicks back, tries another, and somewhere in that second click realises every door has a tax on it.
